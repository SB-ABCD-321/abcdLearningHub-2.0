Chapter 01 — Foundations

Mouse & Computer Interface

Understanding how to precisely control your mouse is the single most important skill in digital art. This chapter covers every mouse action you'll use in Paint, and explains exactly what's happening when you do each one.

Understanding Mouse Input

Your mouse communicates with the computer using two main types of signals: position (where the cursor is on screen) and button state (which buttons are pressed). Paint constantly reads both to know what you're drawing.

📌
Note — How Paint Reads Your Mouse

Paint samples your mouse position hundreds of times per second. When you move the mouse slowly, it records many points close together (smooth curves). When you move fast, points are farther apart (jagged lines). This is why drawing slowly gives better results!

Beginner

Left Click

Press and release the left button quickly. Used to select tools, pick colors, and place single dots on the canvas.

🖱️ Primary drawing button
Beginner

Right Click

Opens a context menu OR draws using Color 2. In Paint, right-clicking while drawing uses your background color instead of foreground.

🖱️ Secondary / Context menu
Core Skill

Click & Drag

Hold the left button down while moving the mouse. This is how you draw freehand lines, resize shapes, and move selections. The most-used technique in Paint.

🖱️ Hold + Move simultaneously
Intermediate

Shift + Drag

Hold Shift while dragging with the Rectangle or Ellipse tool. This constrains the shape to a perfect square or circle. Also constrains lines to 45° angles.

Shift + click & drag
Intermediate

Scroll Wheel

Roll the scroll wheel to move the canvas view up and down. Hold Ctrl while scrolling to zoom in and out of the canvas.

Ctrl + scroll = zoom
Advanced

Precise Positioning

Watch the status bar at the bottom of Paint — it shows your exact cursor coordinates (X, Y pixels). Use this to place shapes and elements with pixel-perfect accuracy.

📐 Check status bar always
💡
Pro Tip — Steady Hand Technique

Rest your wrist lightly on the desk and move from your elbow, not your wrist. This gives you much smoother, more controlled lines — the same technique professional digital artists use! For very fine details, zoom in using Ctrl + scroll up first.

📓 Chapter Notes

1
Color 1 vs Color 2

Paint always tracks two colors simultaneously. Color 1 (foreground) is used when you left-click. Color 2 (background) is used when you right-click with most tools, and by the Eraser. This means you can switch between two colors instantly without clicking the palette!

2
Mouse Sensitivity Matters

If your lines feel shaky, try lowering mouse speed in Windows Settings → Bluetooth & devices → Mouse → Additional mouse settings. A DPI setting of 800–1200 is ideal for drawing in Paint.

3
The Escape Key is Your Friend

Press Esc at any time to cancel a shape you're drawing before you release the mouse. For text, Esc cancels the text entry. Much faster than using Undo!

Note — Reading the Status Bar

The status bar at the bottom constantly updates. It shows: (1) your mouse cursor's current X,Y position in pixels, (2) the size of whatever you're drawing, (3) the canvas size, and (4) the current zoom level. Professional artists watch this constantly to be precise.

Navigating the Canvas

1
Zoom In for Detail

Use Ctrl + + or hold Ctrl and scroll up to zoom in. You can also use the slider in the bottom-right corner of Paint. Zoom in when drawing fine details like text or thin lines.

✨ Working at 400–800% zoom lets you edit individual pixels!
2
Pan the Canvas

When zoomed in, you can't see the whole drawing. Use the scrollbars on the right and bottom edges of the canvas to pan around. Or hold the scroll wheel button and drag.

3
Full-Screen View

Go to View tab → Full screen to see your drawing without any toolbars. Press Esc to return to normal view. Great for checking your overall composition.

4
Resize the Canvas

Go to Home tab → Resize (or press Ctrl + W) to change the canvas size. You can resize by percentage or by exact pixels. Always set the canvas size BEFORE you start drawing!

✨ Standard canvas sizes: 800×600 (screen), 1920×1080 (HD), 595×842 (A4 print)

📓 Chapter Notes

1
Canvas Size & Resolution

The canvas size in Paint is measured in pixels. If you plan to print, use at least 2480 × 3508 pixels for A4 paper at 300 DPI (high quality). If it's just for screen, 1920 × 1080 is plenty. A bigger canvas = a bigger file size!

2
The Ribbon Can Be Hidden

If you want more canvas space, you can minimize the Ribbon by double-clicking any tab. Click a tab again to temporarily see it. Use Ctrl + F1 to toggle it permanently.

3
Multiple Windows

You can open two Paint windows at the same time (run Paint twice). This lets you reference one drawing while working on another — a common technique for copying designs!

Chapter 03 — Foundations

Complete Tool Reference

An in-depth look at every tool in MS Paint — not just what it does, but exactly how to use it with step-by-step instructions and professional tips for each one.

✏️

Pencil

Shortcut: P

Draws 1-pixel wide freehand lines. The classic drawing tool — what you use most for sketching.

  1. Select Pencil from the toolbar or press P
  2. Click Color 1 to choose your drawing color
  3. Click and drag on the canvas to draw
  4. Right-click and drag to draw with Color 2
⚡ Pencil ignores brush size settings — it's always 1 pixel. Use Brush for thicker lines.
🖌️

Brush (Paintbrush)

Shortcut: B

Draws smooth, soft lines in various shapes and sizes. Choose from 9 different brush tip styles in the Ribbon.

  1. Select Brush or press B
  2. In the Ribbon, click a brush shape (round, calligraphy, oil, etc.)
  3. Choose a size (small, medium, large, extra-large)
  4. Click and drag to paint
⚡ Try the "Oil" or "Marker" brush styles for different artistic effects!
🪣

Fill with Color

Shortcut: F

Fills a closed area with your chosen color instantly. Think of pouring paint into a container.

  1. Select Fill or press F
  2. Choose a color from the palette
  3. Click anywhere inside a closed shape
  4. The entire enclosed area fills with color
⚠️ If there's even a 1-pixel gap in the outline, fill will "leak" outside. Zoom in to check for gaps!
💉

Color Picker (Eyedropper)

Shortcut: I

Picks up the exact color from any pixel on your canvas and sets it as your active drawing color.

  1. Select Color Picker or press I
  2. Left-click any pixel to set it as Color 1
  3. Right-click any pixel to set it as Color 2
  4. Your previous tool is restored automatically
⚡ Quick tip: Hold Ctrl while any tool is active to temporarily switch to the Color Picker!
⬛

Eraser

Shortcut: E

Erases pixels and replaces them with Color 2 (usually white). Can also be used as a "painting with Color 2" tool.

  1. Select Eraser or press E
  2. Choose a size in the Ribbon (small to extra-large)
  3. Left-click and drag to erase (fills with Color 2)
  4. Right-click and drag to erase ONLY Color 1, leaving everything else
⚡ Right-click erasing is a powerful technique — it selectively removes one specific color from complex drawings!
📏

Line Tool

Shortcut: L

Draws perfectly straight lines between two points. Essential for technical drawings and geometric art.

  1. Select Line or press L
  2. Choose line thickness in the Ribbon (1px to 8px)
  3. Click where the line should start
  4. Drag to the endpoint and release
⚡ Hold Shift while dragging to snap to exactly 0°, 45°, or 90° angles!
📐

Curve Tool

Shortcut: U

Draws smooth Bezier curves. Unlike freehand, these curves are mathematically perfect and adjustable.

  1. Select Curve or press U
  2. Click and drag to draw the base line
  3. Click once and drag to set the first curve point
  4. Click again and drag to set the second curve point
⚠️ Curve tool requires exactly 3 clicks to complete. Practice until it feels natural — it's very powerful once mastered!
⬜

Rectangle

Shortcut: R

Draws rectangles. In the Ribbon choose: outline only, filled with outline, or filled without outline.

  1. Select Rectangle or press R
  2. Choose fill style in the Ribbon
  3. Click one corner and drag to the opposite corner
  4. Release to place the shape
⚡ Hold Shift to draw a perfect square. The shape outline uses Color 1, fill uses Color 2!
⭕

Ellipse (Oval/Circle)

Shortcut: O

Draws ellipses and circles. Works the same as Rectangle but draws an oval within the bounding box.

  1. Select Ellipse or press O
  2. Choose fill style in the Ribbon
  3. Click and drag to define the bounding box
  4. Hold Shift for a perfect circle
⚡ To draw a circle from its center, start dragging from the middle of where you want the circle to be.
📐

Triangle / Polygon

Shortcut: G

Draws triangles and multi-sided polygons by clicking each corner point. Supports 3 to any number of sides.

  1. Select Polygon or press G
  2. Click the first corner
  3. Click each subsequent corner
  4. Double-click to close the shape automatically
⚡ Hold Shift between clicks to keep sides at perfect 45° or 90° angles!
🔤

Text Tool

Shortcut: T

Adds text to your drawing. Once confirmed, text is permanently part of the image and cannot be edited separately.

  1. Select Text or press T
  2. Click and drag to create a text box
  3. Choose font, size, and bold/italic in the Ribbon
  4. Type your text, then click outside the box to confirm
⚠️ IMPORTANT: Text becomes "burned in" as pixels once you click away or switch tools. If you need to edit it, use Ctrl+Z immediately!
🔲

Select (Rectangle)

Shortcut: S

Selects a rectangular area of the canvas. Once selected, you can move, copy, cut, resize, or delete the selection.

  1. Select the Rectangle Select tool or press S
  2. Drag to draw a selection box around what you want
  3. Drag the selection to move it
  4. Use Ctrl+C to copy or Delete to erase
⚡ Enable "Transparent selection" in the Ribbon to make the white background of your pasted object transparent!
⭐
Pro Note — Shape Fill Modes Explained

For Rectangle, Ellipse, and Polygon tools, the Ribbon shows 3 fill options: (1) Outline only — draws just the border in Color 1. (2) Outline + Fill — border in Color 1, filled with Color 2. (3) Fill only — solid shape in Color 1, no border. Understanding this lets you combine colors creatively without needing to fill separately.

📓 Chapter Notes

1
Why Tools Matter More Than You Think

Using the right tool saves enormous time. Drawing a circle freehand with the Pencil vs. using the Ellipse tool (holding Shift) is the difference between 2 minutes of struggling and 2 seconds of perfection. Always ask: "Is there a tool that does this automatically?"

2
The Curve Tool — Most Underused

Most beginners ignore the Curve tool, but it's one of the most powerful. It creates smooth Bezier curves — the same math used in professional vector software. Practice it by drawing wave shapes and S-curves.

3
Tool Memory

Paint remembers the last settings for each tool. If you switch from Brush back to Eraser, it remembers the eraser size you last used. Use this to your advantage — configure each tool optimally once and it stays that way!

Chapter 04 — Core Skills

Colors & Color Theory

Color is the most powerful element in any artwork. This chapter teaches both how to use Paint's color tools and the fundamental principles of color theory that make drawings look professional.

The Color Palette in Paint

Paint comes with 20 preset colors displayed in the palette. You can customize any of the 10 bottom-row colors by double-clicking them to open the custom color picker.

Understanding Color Relationships

Colors that work well together follow mathematical rules. Here are the four main harmony types:

Complementary

Colors opposite on the wheel. High contrast, very vibrant together. (Red + Cyan)

Analogous

Colors next to each other. Natural, harmonious, pleasing. (Red, Orange, Yellow)

Triadic

Three colors evenly spaced. Colorful and balanced. (Red, Green, Blue)

Monochromatic

One color in different shades. Elegant and sophisticated. (Dark gray → White)

Custom Colors — The Color Picker

Double-click any color swatch in the palette to open the "Edit Colors" dialog. This is one of Paint's most powerful features.

📌
Note — RGB vs HSL

In the Edit Colors dialog, you can enter colors two ways. RGB (Red, Green, Blue) — each goes from 0–255. RGB(255,0,0) = pure red. RGB(0,0,0) = black. RGB(255,255,255) = white. Hue/Sat/Lum — more intuitive: Hue = which color (0–239), Saturation = how vivid (0–240), Luminance = how bright (0–240).

Technique

Skin Tone Mixing

Skin tones are NOT just "pink" or "brown". Mix: high Red, medium Green, low Blue. Try RGB(255, 210, 170) for light skin, RGB(180, 120, 80) for medium, RGB(100, 60, 30) for dark.

Technique

Shading Without Gray

Instead of adding black to shade, add the color's complement. Shadow on blue → add a hint of dark orange. This gives richer, more vibrant shadows than plain gray mixing.

Advanced

Saving Custom Colors

The bottom 10 squares in Paint's palette are "custom colors" — double-click one, mix your perfect color, click "Add to Custom Colors". It stays saved as long as Paint is open.

⚠️
Warning — Transparent Background

MS Paint does not natively support transparency (alpha channel). When you erase, you get white — not transparency. To work with transparent PNGs, save as PNG and note that the "white" background will not be transparent. Use the transparent selection mode when pasting to work around this limitation.

📓 Chapter Notes

1
Hex Color Codes

Every color has a hex code — a 6-digit code starting with #. Example: #FF0000 = red, #0078D4 = Microsoft blue. Paint doesn't show hex codes directly, but if you know an RGB value, you can convert: each pair of hex digits = one RGB number. R=255 = FF, G=120 = 78, B=212 = D4.

2
The 60-30-10 Rule

Professional designers use this rule: 60% of your artwork uses a dominant color, 30% uses a secondary color, and 10% uses an accent color. Try applying this to any drawing and notice how much more balanced it looks!

3
White is NOT the Background Default

You can change Paint's default canvas color! Before drawing, press Ctrl+A to select all (gets you the white canvas), then use Fill to flood it with any color. Now you're drawing on a colored background — great for night scenes or stylized art.

Chapter 05 — Core Skills

Drawing Techniques

Tools alone don't make art — technique does. This chapter covers the systematic approaches professional artists use to create clean, detailed, expressive drawings in MS Paint.

Fundamental Techniques

Technique: Pixel Art

Advanced
Zoom: 800%

Pixel art is drawing where every individual pixel is deliberately placed. Paint is perfect for this — it was originally designed for this exact purpose!

  1. Zoom to 800% or more using Ctrl + scroll wheel
  2. Use only the Pencil tool (1px width)
  3. Work on a small canvas: 32×32 or 64×64 pixels
  4. Use Fill tool for solid color areas
  5. Zoom out with Ctrl+0 to see the final result

Technique: Layering with Shapes

Intermediate

Complex scenes are built by stacking simple shapes on top of each other — background first, foreground last.

  1. Draw the sky background first using a filled rectangle
  2. Add the ground with another filled rectangle on top
  3. Draw far objects (mountains, buildings) next
  4. Add closer objects (trees, people) last
  5. Use Fill tool to quickly color large areas

Technique: Creating Gradients

Advanced

Paint has no gradient tool, but you can fake gradients by painting horizontal lines with gradually changing colors.

  1. Pick your starting color from the palette
  2. Draw a horizontal line across the top of the area
  3. Pick a slightly different color (adjust RGB by ~10)
  4. Draw the next line just below, repeat
  5. Use Ctrl+Z if a color jump looks too sharp

Advanced Shading Technique

⭐
Pro Technique — Dithering

Dithering creates the illusion of a third color using only two. Example: alternating red and white pixels creates a "pink" that doesn't actually exist in your palette. Zoom to 800% and use the Pencil to place pixels in a checkerboard pattern between two colors. This was the technique used in 8-bit video games and creates beautiful, retro-style gradients.

1
Rule of Thirds — Composition

Imagine your canvas divided into a 3×3 grid. Place the most important elements where the grid lines intersect. This is the single most powerful rule in visual art and photography, and it works in Paint too.

✨ Enable the grid in Paint: View tab → Show → Grid lines
2
Sketch First, Refine Later

Use the Pencil with a light gray color to rough out your composition first. Then trace over your sketch with clean colors and shapes. This prevents you from getting "stuck" on details before the overall layout is right.

3
Use Shapes as Guides

Draw a lightly colored ellipse or rectangle where a complex object will go, then draw on top of it and delete the guide shape with the Eraser or Undo. Guides are a professional technique for placing proportional objects.

📓 Chapter Notes

1
The Grid is Your Best Friend

Turn on the grid in Paint's View tab when doing pixel art or technical drawings. You can also zoom to exactly 100% and use the grid to count pixels precisely. The grid disappears when you zoom out below 400%.

2
Symmetry Trick

To draw a perfectly symmetrical face or object: draw one half, select it with the Rectangle Select tool, Ctrl+C to copy, Ctrl+V to paste, then go to Home → Image → Flip horizontal. Position the flipped copy next to the original. Perfect symmetry every time!

3
Canvas Size vs Drawing Size

The canvas size and what you draw on it are independent. You can draw a tiny 50×50 pixel character on a 1920×1080 canvas. When done, use Home → Image → Crop to trim the canvas exactly to your drawing boundaries.

Chapter 06 — Core Skills

Selection & Editing

The ability to select, move, copy, and transform parts of your drawing is what separates a beginner from an intermediate artist. Master these techniques to work non-destructively.

Selection Tools

Beginner

Rectangle Select

Drag to select any rectangular region. The marching ants (dashed border) show your selection. Use Ctrl+A to select everything.

S or Ctrl+A for all
Intermediate

Free-Form Select

Draw any irregular shape around what you want to select. Paint automatically closes the selection shape when you release the mouse. Great for selecting non-rectangular objects.

Found in the selection dropdown
Advanced

Transparent Selection

Enable this in the Selection dropdown before pasting. White pixels in the pasted image become transparent, letting the background show through. Essential for compositing multiple drawings.

Selection → Transparent selection

Transformations

→
Move a Selection

After selecting, hover over the selection until the cursor shows a 4-arrow move icon, then drag. The area left behind becomes white (filled with background color).

✨ Hold Shift while moving to snap to 45° angles
⤢
Resize a Selection

After selecting, drag any of the 8 handles (corners and edges) to resize. The selection content stretches to fit. This is how you scale elements up or down.

⚠️ Scaling up makes pixels visible and blurry — always work at the largest size and scale down
↔
Flip & Rotate

Go to Home → Image group → Rotate or Flip. Options: Rotate 90° (clockwise/counter-clockwise), Rotate 180°, Flip horizontal, Flip vertical. This applies to the whole canvas OR just the selection.

✂
Crop to Selection

Make a selection, then go to Home → Crop. Paint trims the entire canvas down to exactly the size of your selection. Perfect for removing unwanted white borders.

✨ Use this to export just part of a larger drawing
⚠️
Warning — Destructive Editing

MS Paint is 100% destructive — every action permanently changes the pixels. There are no "layers" or "history panel" beyond Undo. This is why frequent Ctrl+S saves with different filenames (Save As) is essential. Many artists save versions: drawing_v1.png, drawing_v2.png, etc. so they can go back.

📓 Chapter Notes

1
The Copy-Paste Workflow

A powerful technique: select a portion of your drawing, Ctrl+C, then Ctrl+V. The pasted copy appears as a floating selection you can position freely. This lets you duplicate elements like windows in a building, petals in a flower, or tiles in a pattern very rapidly.

2
Paste From File

You can paste an image file directly from your computer into Paint: Home tab → Clipboard → Paste → Paste from. This lets you combine multiple photos or drawings into one composition — a simple form of photo collaging!

Chapter 08 — Advanced

Pro-Level Techniques

These advanced workflows go beyond basic drawing. These are the techniques that make the difference between a beginner's drawing and one that looks polished and intentional.

Advanced Workflows

Advanced

Version Control System

Save your work every 10–15 minutes with a new filename: myart_v01.png, myart_v02.png. Never overwrite. This "manual versioning" protects against mistakes and lets you see your progress over time.

Advanced

The Zoom-In Workflow

Work at 200–400% for most drawing. Zoom to 800%+ for fine details and corrections. Zoom to 50% or Ctrl+0 to check the overall composition. Professional artists constantly switch zoom levels.

Intermediate

Stamp & Repeat

Draw one element (like a leaf or star), select it with Ctrl+A if it's the whole canvas (or use Rectangle Select), copy it, paste it, then reposition. Paste as many times as needed. All copies stay consistent.

Advanced

Color Replacement

Use the Eraser tool with right-click dragging. Set Color 1 to the color you want to REMOVE, and Color 2 to the new color. The eraser will only erase pixels that match Color 1, leaving everything else intact.

Advanced

Tracing from a Photo

Open a photo in Paint (Ctrl+O), then draw directly on top of it — tracing the outlines with the Pencil or Brush. When done, use Fill tool to add flat colors. Delete photo sections by painting over with white or your background color.

Advanced

Tile Pattern Creation

Design a small pattern (e.g., 64×64 pixels) that tiles seamlessly. Then paste it repeatedly side by side to fill a large canvas. Useful for backgrounds, textures, wrapping paper designs.

📌
Note — Paint's Limitations (and Workarounds)

MS Paint lacks: layers, transparency, vector shapes, and filters. Workarounds: (1) Simulate layers by compositing images using Paste From. (2) Use PNG format for transparency support in other apps. (3) Use the Curve tool for smooth vector-like shapes. (4) Open the result in Paint 3D (built into Windows) for 3D effects.

📓 Chapter Notes

1
Paint vs. Professional Software

Paint teaches you the same fundamentals used in Photoshop, GIMP, and Krita — just without the advanced features. Every concept you learn here (selection, fill, color theory, layers as manual compositing) translates directly to professional software. Think of MS Paint as "training wheels" that build real, transferable skills.

2
Screenshot Editing

Press Print Screen (PrtScn) on your keyboard, open Paint, press Ctrl+V — the screenshot pastes directly as your canvas! This is how many people use Paint to annotate screenshots for school projects or presentations. Add text with the T tool, draw arrows with the Line tool, highlight with a semi-transparent colored rectangle.

Always Save as PNG for Digital Art

JPEG compression introduces artifacts — blurry pixels and color smearing around sharp edges. This is catastrophic for pixel art and text. PNG is lossless: what you drew is EXACTLY what you get. Only use JPEG for photographs where the artifacts are invisible.

Print Settings

1
Set Canvas to Print Size First

Use Ctrl+E (Image Properties) to set width/height in inches or centimeters instead of pixels. For A4: 8.27 × 11.69 inches. For US Letter: 8.5 × 11 inches. This ensures your print matches your screen design.

✨ For crisp printing, set resolution to 300 DPI in properties
2
Print Preview

Press Ctrl+P and click "Preview" (or use the Page Setup option) to see how your drawing will look on paper before printing. Check that nothing is cut off at the edges.

Chapter 11 — Reference

Glossary of Terms

Key vocabulary every computer art student should know. These terms appear in Paint documentation, tutorials, and are used across all digital art software.

Essential Digital Art Vocabulary

Pixel Core
The smallest unit of a digital image. "Pixel" = "Picture Element". At 100% zoom, each square dot you see is one pixel.
Resolution Core
How many pixels are in an image, expressed as Width × Height (e.g. 1920×1080). More pixels = more detail = larger file size.
Canvas Core
The drawing area in Paint — the white space where your artwork lives. Named after the fabric artists paint on in real life.
Raster Technical
Image type made of pixels (like Paint). Zooming in makes it blurry/pixelated. Opposite of vector. Most photos are raster.
Vector Technical
Image type made of mathematical paths, not pixels. Scales to any size without blurring. Paint is NOT vector. Illustrator, Inkscape are vector tools.
DPI Print
Dots Per Inch — how densely packed pixels are when printed. Screen: 72–96 DPI. Print: 300 DPI. Higher DPI = sharper printed image.
RGB Color
Red, Green, Blue — the three primary colors of light used by screens. Every color is a mix of these three values (0–255 each).
Hex Code Color
A 6-digit code for a color, starting with #. Example: #FF0000 = red. Used in web design and digital art universally.
Transparency Editing
How "see-through" a pixel is. 0% = invisible, 100% = fully solid. Paint doesn't fully support this — use PNG format for partial support.
Layer Editing
A separate drawing surface stacked above/below others, like sheets of transparent film. Paint doesn't have layers — this is its biggest limitation.
Lossless Format
File format that preserves every pixel exactly (PNG, BMP). Use for digital art. No quality loss when saving/reopening.
Lossy Format
File format that discards some data to reduce size (JPEG). Causes compression artifacts. Fine for photos, bad for art.
Bezier Curve Technical
A mathematically perfect smooth curve, controlled by "anchor points". Paint's Curve tool draws Bezier curves used in all professional design software.
Dithering Advanced
Simulating a color by alternating two other colors in a pattern. Used in pixel art to create smooth gradients with a limited color palette.
Crop Editing
Cutting away the edges of an image to focus on a specific part or change its dimensions. Paint's crop function removes canvas area outside the selection.
Clipboard Core
Temporary computer memory that stores your copied/cut data. Ctrl+C copies to clipboard, Ctrl+V pastes from it. Only holds one item at a time.
